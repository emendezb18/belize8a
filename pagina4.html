The beutifull mahogany tree (7)

If you don't like the ocean, then you can enjoy Belize's majestic forests, where you can find beautiful flora and fauna, and home to the mahogany tree, a mighty tree that gave a valuable red dye to British colonizers and made the belize flag.

Belize after being colonized was used for the exotic materials it had, in those materials was the mahogany tree, a tree so tall and with a beautiful red wood that it was widely used for the uniforms of the English. Although Belize belonged to the British, the Portuguese wanted the wood from the mahogany tree, so they went to Brazil to find a replacement.

But you might be wondering what that has to do with the flag. Well on the flag there are two black men who were slaves and were engaged in cutting down mahogany trees and they represented the hard work they had to go through, while in the center there is a mahogany tree that represents what started the economy in belize. In conclusion, this tree, being a plant, represents a lot of the pain that Belize had to go through to finally be free.

The Bair's tapir inhabits the tropical forests of Central and South America. In addition to eating the twigs and growth tips of a variety of understory plants, they also use their jaws to snap young trees to reach towering plants.

Although they use whistles and screeches to signal when invaders are approaching your domain. Feeds on grasses, aquatic vegetation, leaves, shoots, and fruits of low-growing shrubs. at night and spend most of their lives completely alone.

But there is much to appreciate about these creatures, Belize's largest indigenous land mammal. In short: the Baird Tapir animal is one of the oldest animals found in Belize and tapirs are vital components in their ecosystems. They are masters at dispersing seeds and getting them well fertilized, providing themselves and other wildlife with continuity.

The Bright Swamp (9)

The phrase "drain the swamp" has gained a lot of popularity in recent years. Swamps and wetlands serve as vital habitat for wildlife, buffers against torrential rains, natural filtration systems, as well as hiding places, obstructions for agriculture and development, and havens for disease-spreading mosquitoes. In conclusion, our connection with wetlands today could be a challenge.

Wetlands were a resource, a place to grow crops for almost 1000 years through a system of wetland agriculture, according to ancient Mayans of Central America. America. A recent study demonstrates the sophistication and scale of Mayan agriculture in the wetlands that choke the swamps to grow food.

This swamp was discovered by a group belonging to the Mayans thousands of years ago, and the Mayans used it for many things. The Bright Swamp was the one they used the most to plant and grow food and to collect rainwater. This swamp is now a normal part of a Belizean forest that still has water in its entirety with different wood included.
